Complete guide to Stock Market Analysis

This guide provides an overview into how to analyze stocks, including the stock metrics that are displayed in the watchlist.

Investopedia

Investing in stocks in difficult, but it is a great long-term strategy for accumulating wealth.

Stocks have historically outperformed other investments, such as bonds, commodities, CDs (Certificates of Deposit), savings accounts, etc.

When investing in stocks, there are two different approaches: passive and active.

Passive investing means buying broad-market index funds or ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds) and holding on to them for the long-term (5+ years).

Active investing means taking a more hands-on approach by picking individual stocks to purchase and trading at a reasonable frequency (not day-trading!).

Combining both approaches is actually a great tool, as some of your assets are tracking major indices (passive investments), while you are also keeping track of individual stocks to purchase/sell based on market conditions and trends.

52-Week High/Low

The 52-week high refers to the highest market price of a given security over a 52-week (one year) period.

The 52-week high serves as an indicator for potential investors. Investor's will often reference the 52-week high for a stock when looking at the current price.

While it may be tempting to think that you should buy a stock when it's close to it's 52-week low and sell when it's close to its 52-week high, this approach isn't guaranteed to work.

In fact, a lot of great stocks will trade near there 52-week high as their stock price continues to rise.

Market Capitalization

Market capitalization (or market cap) is the total value of the tradable shares of a publicly traded company; it is equal to the share price times the number of shares outstanding. As outstanding stock is bought and sold in public markets, capitalization could be used as a proxy for the public opinion of a company's net worth and is a determining factor in some forms of stock valuation. Preferred shares are included in the calculation.

Rule of thumb for different cap classes:
  • Mega-cap: Over $200 billion
  • Large-cap: Over $10 billion
  • Mid-cap: $2 billion–$10 billion
  • Small-cap: $250 million–$2 billion
  • Micro-cap: Below $250 million
  • Nano-cap: Below $50 million

Dividends

Dividends are payments that a company makes to its shareholders (people that own its stock). It is a good alternative to generate passive income.

Typically, larger and well-established companies will pay out dividends.

There is a sub-category of investing that focuses entirely on investing in dividend-paying stocks for long-term investing.

A quick metric to check for a company is the Dividend Yield, which is a financial ratio that tells you the percentage of a company's share price that it pays out in dividends each year.

Dividend yield = (Annual Dividend per share)/ (Current share price) * 100

Price-to-Earnings (PE) Ratio

The price-to-earnings ratio (P/E ratio) is the ratio for valuing a company that measures its current share price relative to its earnings per share (EPS). The price-to-earnings ratio is also sometimes known as the price multiple or the earnings multiple.

P/E ratios are used by investors and analysts to determine the relative value of a company's shares in an apples-to-apples comparison. It can also be used to compare a company against its own historical record or to compare aggregate markets against one another or over time.

Usually a company with a PE Ratio of or below 20 is considered undervalued whereas a company with a PE Ratio of or above 40 is considered overvalued

More information: Forbes - What Is the PE Ratio?

Return On Equity (ROE) Ratio

ROE is a profitability ratiothat signifies the rate of return a shareholder receives for the portion of their investment in that company. It measures how well a company generates positive returns to its shareholders’ investment.

Since profit is an actual driver of stock prices, separating out the profits earned with shareholder equity is actually a pretty good indicator of the financial health of a company and fair value of its stock.

ROE is a gauge of a corporation's profitability and how efficiently it generates those profits.

More information: Forbes - What Is the ROE Ratio?

Price-to-Earnings-Growth (PEG) Ratio

The PEG Ratio takes the P/E Ratio and incorporates the expected earnings growth to provide a valuation of a company.

At a high-level, a PEG Ratio can be interpreted as:

  • < 1.0 - indicates that a stock is undervalued
  • > 1.0 - indicates a stock is overvalued

However, the PEG ratio can vary from industry to industry and it fluctuates based on overall market conditions as well.

It's best to use the PEG ratio when comparing companies in similar industries (ie. Home Depot vs. Lowes)

More information: Forbes - What Is the PEG Ratio?

Price-to-Book (PB) Ratio

The book value of a company is the value of its assets minus its liabilities (which can be found in a company's balance sheet).

One of the key concepts of value investing is finding stocks that are under-priced. The book value of a company is often used to find under-priced stocks.

The Price-to-Book Value ratio is a good metric for comparing companies in similar industries.

Debt-to-Equity (DE) Ratio

The debt-to-equity (D/E) ratio is used to evaluate a company's financial leverage and is calculated by dividing a company’s total liabilities by its shareholder equity.

It is a measure of the degree to which a company is financing its operations through debt versus wholly owned funds.

However, the D/E ratio is difficult to compare across industry groups where ideal amounts of debt will vary.

Investors will often modify the D/E ratio to focus on long-term debt only because the risks associated with long-term liabilities are different than short-term debt and payables.

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

This is the portion of a company’s profit that is assigned to each share of its stock. It’s essentially the bottom line net income, just on a per-share basis.

A growing EPS is a good sign to investors because it means that their shares are likely to be worth more.

We can calculate the earnings per share of a company by dividing its total profit by the number of outstanding shares.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

In simplest terms, Free Cash Flow is the cash left over after a company has paid for its operating expenses and capital expenditures.

Companies with high free cash flow can improve shareholder value, fund innovation, and survive downturns better than their less-liquid counterparts.

Many investors cherish FCF as a fundamental indicator because it shows whether a company still has enough cash to reward its shareholders through dividends after funding operations and capital expenditures.

Profit Margin

Profit margin indicates the percentage of sales that get turned into profits. This metric gauges how well a company is making money.

This metric quickly indicates if a company is profitable or not. As with the PEG Ratio, profit margins vary by industry.
